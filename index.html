Find Focal Terms

Patent–Paper Terminological & Semantic Overlap Analysis

Do Patents Speak the Same Language as Science?

This project investigates the terminological and semantic overlap between biomedical patents and their cited scientific papers. It identifies focal terms — words that appear in both a patent and its cited literature — measures how many such terms each patent shares, and tests whether those terms carry the same meaning in both contexts.

Three-Task Pipeline

01

Identify Focal Terms

Extract terms from patents and cited papers. Inner-join on (patent_id, term) — any term present in both is a focal term.

02

Measure Overlap Intensity

Count unique focal terms per patent and describe the distribution with summary statistics, histograms, and density plots.

03

Semantic Comparison

Embed each focal term's context in both documents using all-MiniLM-L6-v2 and compute cosine similarity to test whether the term means the same thing in both.

Task 2 — Overlap Intensity

Task 3 — Semantic Context Comparison

Goal: Assess whether focal terms are used in similar or different semantic contexts in patents vs. scientific papers. Sentence embeddings (all-MiniLM-L6-v2) were generated for each focal term's context in both documents. Cosine similarity measures context alignment. Scores range from −1 to 1, where 1 = identical context and 0 = unrelated.

Key Finding

High similarity correlates with domain-specific drug names, enzymes, and clinical terms — words with narrow, stable meanings used identically in both domains. Low similarity occurs for generic function words (first, second, group) whose co-occurrence is coincidental.

The mean of 0.375 is lower than the small sample (0.441), reflecting greater domain diversity. High similarity for specific pharmacological and chemical terms; low similarity for generic stop-word-like terms (known, following, up) that carry no shared semantic content.
